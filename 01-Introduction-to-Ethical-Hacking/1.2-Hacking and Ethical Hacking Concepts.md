# Hacking Concepts

## Hacking

Hacking is the process of identifying weaknesses in computer systems, networks, applications, or security mechanisms and exploiting them to gain unauthorized access or perform malicious activities.

### Primary Goals of Attackers

* Unauthorized access
* Data theft
* Information disclosure
* Privilege escalation
* Financial gain
* Service disruption
* Reputation damage
* Espionage

### Common Hacker Motivations

* Curiosity/challenge
* Fame or recognition
* Political motives
* Revenge
* Financial profit
* Corporate espionage
* Cyber warfare
* Hacktivism

---

# Categories of Hackers

## White Hat Hacker

Also called:

* Ethical Hacker
* Security Analyst
* Penetration Tester

### Characteristics

* Authorized to test systems
* Works within legal scope
* Helps improve security posture
* Uses hacking techniques defensively

### Key Exam Point

White hats perform attacks with **written permission** from the organization.

---

## Black Hat Hacker

### Characteristics

* Unauthorized attacker
* Malicious intent
* Steals, destroys, manipulates, or disrupts systems
* Violates confidentiality, integrity, and availability

### Typical Activities

* Malware deployment
* Credential theft
* Data exfiltration
* Ransomware attacks
* Botnet operations

### MCQ Trigger

Unauthorized + malicious + illegal = Black Hat

---

## Gray Hat Hacker

### Characteristics

* Operates between white hat and black hat
* May violate laws or access systems without permission
* Usually not primarily financially motivated
* Often exposes vulnerabilities publicly

### Key Difference

Gray hats may hack without permission but may not intend direct damage.

---

## Script Kiddie

### Characteristics

* Limited technical skills
* Uses ready-made tools/scripts
* Relies on publicly available exploit kits
* Often performs simple attacks

### Typical Tools

* Automated exploit frameworks
* Preconfigured malware kits
* Public scanning tools

### MCQ Association

Low skill + borrowed tools = Script Kiddie

---

## Suicide Hacker

### Characteristics

* Does not care about being identified
* Often ideologically motivated
* May conduct destructive attacks
* Little concern for legal consequences

---

## Hacktivist

### Characteristics

* Politically or socially motivated
* Targets governments, corporations, or institutions
* Uses hacking for activism

### Common Activities

* Website defacement
* Data leaks
* DDoS attacks
* Information exposure

### Named Group Examples

* Anonymous
* LulzSec

---

## State-Sponsored Hacker

### Characteristics

* Backed by governments
* Conducts cyber espionage or cyber warfare
* Targets critical infrastructure and sensitive data

### Common Targets

* Defense systems
* Energy sector
* Banking systems
* Government agencies

---

## Cyber Terrorist

### Characteristics

* Intends to create fear or panic
* Attacks critical national infrastructure
* Motivated by ideological or extremist goals

### Typical Targets

* Power grids
* Transportation systems
* Healthcare systems
* Military networks

# Ethical Hacking Concepts

## Ethical Hacking

Ethical hacking is the authorized practice of bypassing system security to identify vulnerabilities and weaknesses before malicious attackers exploit them.

### Purpose

* Improve security posture
* Discover vulnerabilities
* Validate existing controls
* Assess risk exposure
* Recommend countermeasures

### Ethical Hacker Responsibilities

* Obtain written authorization
* Define scope clearly
* Maintain confidentiality
* Report findings responsibly
* Avoid damaging systems
* Follow legal and organizational requirements

# Information Security Principles Relevant to Ethical Hacking

## CIA Triad

### Confidentiality

Protects information from unauthorized disclosure.

#### Common Controls

* Encryption
* Access control
* Authentication
* Data classification

---

### Integrity

Ensures data accuracy and trustworthiness.

#### Common Controls

* Hashing
* Digital signatures
* Checksums
* File integrity monitoring

---

### Availability

Ensures systems and data remain accessible.

#### Threat Examples

* DoS/DDoS attacks
* Ransomware
* Hardware failure

#### Common Controls

* Redundancy
* Backups
* Disaster recovery
* Load balancing

---

# Key Security Terms

## Threat

A potential cause of unwanted impact.

### Examples

* Malware
* Insider attacks
* Phishing
* Misconfiguration

---

## Vulnerability

A weakness that can be exploited.

### Examples

* Weak passwords
* Unpatched systems
* Open ports
* Improper access control

---

## Exploit

A method or code that takes advantage of a vulnerability.

### Relationship Mapping

Vulnerability + exploit = successful attack opportunity

---

## Payload

The malicious component delivered after exploitation.

### Examples

* Ransomware
* Reverse shell
* Keylogger
* Trojan

---

## Risk

Probability of a threat exploiting a vulnerability and causing damage.

### Basic Relationship

Risk = Threat × Vulnerability × Impact

# Types of Cyber Attacks

## Passive Attack

### Characteristics

* Monitors systems without modifying data
* Difficult to detect
* Focuses on information gathering

### Examples

* Sniffing
* Traffic analysis
* Eavesdropping

### Main Impact

Confidentiality

---

## Active Attack

### Characteristics

* Alters, disrupts, or damages systems/data
* Easier to detect

### Examples

* DoS attacks
* Data modification
* Malware injection
* Session hijacking

### Main Impact

Integrity and availability

---

# Insider vs Outsider Attacks

| Attack Type     | Characteristics                                         |
| --------------- | ------------------------------------------------------- |
| Insider Attack  | Initiated by trusted individuals with legitimate access |
| Outsider Attack | Initiated by unauthorized external attackers            |

### Insider Threat Examples

* Employees
* Contractors
* Vendors
* Business partners

### MCQ Focus

Insiders already possess some level of authorized access.

# Cyber Kill Chain Concept

CEH commonly tests understanding of attack progression.

## Typical Attack Flow

1. Reconnaissance
2. Scanning and Enumeration
3. Gaining Access
4. Maintaining Access
5. Covering Tracks

### Purpose

Shows how attackers systematically compromise targets.

### Important Mapping

| Phase              | Objective                    |
| ------------------ | ---------------------------- |
| Reconnaissance     | Gather information           |
| Scanning           | Identify live hosts/services |
| Gaining Access     | Exploit vulnerabilities      |
| Maintaining Access | Persistence/backdoors        |
| Covering Tracks    | Avoid detection              |

# Ethical Hacking Phases

## Reconnaissance

Information gathering phase.

### Examples

* WHOIS lookup
* DNS footprinting
* Social engineering
* Search engine footprinting

---

## Scanning

Identifies active systems and services.

### Examples

* Port scanning
* Vulnerability scanning
* Network mapping

### Common Tools

* Nmap
* Nessus

---

## Gaining Access

Exploiting vulnerabilities to enter target systems.

### Examples

* Password attacks
* Exploit execution
* Web application attacks

### Common Tools

* Metasploit
* Hydra

---

## Maintaining Access

Ensures continued access.

### Examples

* Trojans
* Rootkits
* Backdoors

---

## Covering Tracks

Removes evidence of compromise.

### Examples

* Clearing logs
* Hiding files
* Timestamp modification

# Ethical Hacker Types and Roles

## Penetration Tester

Simulates attacks to identify vulnerabilities.

## Security Auditor

Evaluates security policies and compliance.

## Red Team

Simulates real-world offensive attacks.

## Blue Team

Defensive security operations.

## Purple Team

Coordinates red and blue teams to improve security.

### Important Comparison

| Team        | Role                          |
| ----------- | ----------------------------- |
| Red Team    | Attack simulation             |
| Blue Team   | Defense and detection         |
| Purple Team | Collaboration and improvement |

# Penetration Testing Concepts

## Black-Box Testing

Tester has no prior knowledge.

### Simulates

External attacker perspective.

---

## White-Box Testing

Tester has full knowledge.

### Includes

* Source code
* Architecture
* Credentials

---

## Gray-Box Testing

Tester has partial knowledge.

### Simulates

Insider or limited-access attacker.

---

# Vulnerability Assessment vs Penetration Testing

| Concept                  | Purpose                            |
| ------------------------ | ---------------------------------- |
| Vulnerability Assessment | Identify and prioritize weaknesses |
| Penetration Testing      | Actively exploit vulnerabilities   |

### Key Difference

Penetration testing attempts exploitation; vulnerability assessment primarily identifies weaknesses.

# Malware Concepts Commonly Associated with Ethical Hacking

## Virus

Requires user action and attaches to files.

## Worm

Self-replicates across networks.

## Trojan

Disguised as legitimate software.

## Ransomware

Encrypts data for ransom.

## Spyware

Secretly monitors user activity.

## Rootkit

Hides malicious processes/files and maintains privileged access.

### High-Value Differences

| Malware    | Key Trait                   |
| ---------- | --------------------------- |
| Virus      | Needs host file/user action |
| Worm       | Self-spreading              |
| Trojan     | Disguised as legitimate     |
| Rootkit    | Hides presence              |
| Ransomware | Encrypts data for payment   |

# Important CEH Distinctions

| Concept Pair                         | Key Difference                                          |
| ------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------- |
| Threat vs Vulnerability              | Threat causes harm; vulnerability is weakness           |
| Exploit vs Payload                   | Exploit gains access; payload performs malicious action |
| Passive vs Active Attack             | Monitoring vs modification/disruption                   |
| White Hat vs Black Hat               | Authorized vs unauthorized                              |
| Vulnerability Assessment vs Pen Test | Identification vs exploitation                          |
| Virus vs Worm                        | User action vs self-propagation                         |
| Authentication vs Authorization      | Identity verification vs permission control             |
